Setting Up an ATS-Friendly CV

In the Swiss market, your CV must serve dual purposes: pass Applicant Tracking System (ATS) screening and impress human recruiters and hiring managers. This guide covers the essential structure, optimization techniques, and best practices for UX professionals.

The Purpose of a CV in the Swiss UX Market

Understanding the role your CV plays in the hiring process helps you optimize it effectively for both automated systems and human reviewers.

Primary Functions

  • ATS Screening: Initial automated filtering based on keywords, skills, and basic qualifications
  • Recruiter Quick Assessment: 30-second scan to determine if you match the role requirements
  • Hiring Manager Evidence: Detailed proof of experience and capabilities for interview decision
  • Interview Preparation: Discussion points and project examples for conversations

Swiss Market Context: Swiss employers typically spend more time reviewing CVs than in other markets. A well-structured, comprehensive CV is expected and appreciated. However, it must still pass initial ATS screening to reach human eyes.

CV vs. Portfolio Balance

  • CV Focus: Skills, experience, impact, and professional trajectory
  • Portfolio Focus: Process, creativity, problem-solving, and visual design skills
  • Complementary relationship: CV gets you the interview, portfolio demonstrates your capabilities

Recommended Structure for a UX CV

This structure is optimized for both ATS parsing and Swiss employer expectations, providing clear hierarchy and easy scanning.

1. Header Section

Essential elements in order:

  • Full name (larger font, clear hierarchy)
  • Professional title/role (e.g., "Senior UX Designer" or "Product Designer")
  • Location (city, country - work permit status if relevant)
  • Phone number (Swiss format preferred)
  • Professional email address
  • LinkedIn profile URL
  • Portfolio website URL

2. Professional Profile Summary

A concise 2-4 line summary that positions you for the target role. This section should immediately communicate your value proposition.

Generic Example (Avoid)

"Passionate UX designer with experience in creating user-friendly designs. I love solving problems and working with teams to create great products."

Targeted Example (Better)

"Senior UX Designer with 5+ years experience in fintech and e-commerce. Proven track record of increasing conversion rates by 25-40% through user research and data-driven design decisions. Expertise in leading cross-functional teams and stakeholder management in agile environments."

3. Core Skills Section

Organize skills into clear categories for both ATS optimization and human scanning:

  • UX Methods: User research, usability testing, information architecture, interaction design
  • Tools & Software: Figma, Sketch, Adobe Creative Suite, Miro, UserTesting
  • Technical Skills: HTML/CSS basics, prototyping, design systems, accessibility (WCAG)
  • Soft Skills: Stakeholder management, workshop facilitation, agile methodology, cross-functional collaboration

4. Professional Experience

The core of your CV, structured for maximum impact and ATS compatibility:

For each role include:

  • Job title
  • Company name
  • Employment dates (month/year format)
  • Location
  • 3-6 bullet points highlighting achievements and impact

5. Education and Certifications

  • Formal education: Degree, institution, graduation year
  • UX certifications: Google UX Certificate, Nielsen Norman Group, etc.
  • Relevant courses: Bootcamps, online programs, workshops

6. Languages

Critical in the Swiss market - use standardized proficiency levels:

  • Native/Mother tongue
  • Fluent/C2: Near-native proficiency
  • Advanced/C1: Professional working proficiency
  • Intermediate/B2: Good working knowledge
  • Basic/A2-B1: Limited working proficiency

ATS Optimization Best Practices

Applicant Tracking Systems are used by most Swiss companies, especially larger organizations. Your CV must be structured to parse correctly while remaining readable for humans.

Formatting Guidelines

ATS-Safe Formatting Rules:

  • Use standard fonts: Arial, Helvetica, or other sans-serif fonts
  • Avoid complex layouts: No multi-column designs, text boxes, or graphics
  • Use standard section headings: "Experience," "Education," "Skills" rather than creative alternatives
  • Consistent date formatting: MM/YYYY or Month YYYY format
  • No headers/footers: Information in these areas may not be parsed
  • Save as .PDF or .DOCX: Check job posting for preferred format

Keyword Optimization Strategy

  • Mirror job description language: Use exact terms from the posting when accurate
  • Include industry-standard terms: Both abbreviated and full versions (UX, User Experience)
  • Avoid keyword stuffing: Integrate terms naturally within context
  • Use synonyms strategically: Different companies may use varying terminology

Common ATS Parsing Issues to Avoid

  • Images and graphics: Cannot be read by most ATS systems
  • Tables for layout: May cause content to be scrambled
  • Unusual characters: Special symbols or icons may cause errors
  • Merged cells: Can confuse parsing algorithms
  • Text in images: Contact information in graphic format won't be captured

Writing Effective Experience Bullets

Your experience bullets are crucial for demonstrating impact and value. Use a structured approach that showcases both process and results.

Formula for Strong Bullets

Action + Context + Method + Result

Start with strong action verbs, provide context, mention your approach, and quantify outcomes when possible.

Action Verbs for UX Roles

  • Research & Analysis: Conducted, analyzed, evaluated, investigated, synthesized
  • Design & Creation: Designed, created, developed, prototyped, crafted
  • Leadership & Collaboration: Led, facilitated, collaborated, mentored, coordinated
  • Improvement & Optimization: Improved, optimized, enhanced, streamlined, increased

Before and After Examples

Weak Example

"Responsible for designing user interfaces for mobile app. Worked with developers and conducted user testing."

Strong Example

"Led UX design for e-commerce mobile app serving 50K+ users, conducting usability testing with 24 participants and iterating designs based on insights, resulting in 32% increase in checkout completion rate."

Generic Example

"Created wireframes and prototypes using Figma. Presented designs to stakeholders."

Impact-Focused Example

"Developed high-fidelity prototypes and user flows for B2B dashboard redesign, presenting to C-level stakeholders and securing approval for $200K development budget while reducing user task completion time by 45%."

Language and Tone for Swiss Market

Swiss employers appreciate clear, professional communication that demonstrates competence without excessive self-promotion.

Professional Tone Guidelines

  • Direct and clear: Avoid flowery language or excessive adjectives
  • Results-oriented: Focus on outcomes and measurable achievements
  • Collaborative emphasis: Show ability to work within teams and with stakeholders
  • Humble confidence: Professional without being boastful

Cultural Adaptations

Swiss preferences:

  • Precision over creativity in CV language
  • Evidence-based statements rather than opinions
  • Structured presentation of information
  • Professional achievements over personal attributes

Common CV Mistakes in UX Applications

Based on recruiter feedback, these are the most frequent errors that prevent UX professionals from advancing in the Swiss hiring process.

Design and Layout Mistakes

Critical errors to avoid:

  • Overly designed CVs: Treating the CV as a design portfolio piece rather than a professional document
  • Poor ATS compatibility: Complex layouts that break when parsed by automated systems
  • Inconsistent formatting: Different fonts, spacing, or alignment throughout the document
  • Too much visual emphasis: Graphics and colors that distract from content

Content and Structure Issues

  • Generic profile summaries: Vague statements that could apply to any UX role
  • Tool-heavy skill lists: Overemphasis on software without methodology context
  • Missing quantifiable results: No metrics or measurable outcomes
  • Unclear job progression: Difficult to understand career growth and increasing responsibility
  • Missing dates or locations: Incomplete information about employment history

Swiss-Specific Oversights

  • No language proficiency information: Critical oversight in multilingual Switzerland
  • Unclear work authorization status: Important for non-EU candidates
  • American-style formatting: Using formats unfamiliar to Swiss recruiters
  • Missing cover letter: Still expected in Swiss application processes

Quality check: Before submitting, test your CV by uploading it to job platforms to see how it parses, and have a non-UX professional read it to ensure clarity and impact.

CV Length and Section Prioritization

Recommended Length by Experience Level

  • Junior (0-2 years): 1-2 pages maximum
  • Mid-level (2-5 years): 2 pages ideal
  • Senior (5+ years): 2-3 pages acceptable
  • Leadership roles: 3 pages maximum, focus on strategic impact

Information Hierarchy

Prioritize information based on what matters most to Swiss UX employers:

  1. Recent relevant experience (last 5-7 years in detail)
  2. Quantifiable achievements and business impact
  3. Technical and methodological skills
  4. Language capabilities
  5. Education and certifications
  6. Earlier career experience (summary format)
